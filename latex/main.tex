\title{Uncertainty-Aware Deep MRI Reconstruction:\\ A Multi-Modal Trustworthiness Analysis}

\author{Samuel Zhang\inst{1}}
\authorrunning{S.\ Zhang}
\institute{Imperial College London, London, UK\\
\email{samuel.zhang25@imperial.ac.uk}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Deep learning greatly accelerates MRI acquisition by reconstructing diagnostic-quality images from undersampled k-space. This can save a significant time per patient yielding in more diagnosis. However, clinical adoption demands trustworthiness beyond aggregate pixel-level metrics. This work presents a systematic evaluation framework probing four trust dimensions of a self-proposed U-Net reconstruction network with learnable data-consistency (DC) layers, trained on cardiac MR images. Explainability is assessed through saliency maps, Grad-CAM, and integrated gradients. Predictive uncertainty is quantified via both Monte-Carlo (MC) Dropout and deep ensembles, with calibration and sparsification comparisons. Robustness is examined under k-space noise, FGSM/PGD adversarial attacks, and MR$\to$CT distribution shift. Downstream clinical impact is measured through cardiac segmentation Dice scores. A DC-layer ablation reveals an 8.1\,dB PSNR collapse at inference, confirming physics-informed consistency as a prerequisite for trustworthy reconstruction. Experiments on the MM-WHS dataset demonstrate that uncertainty maps reliably flag degraded reconstructions across all perturbation modes.

\keywords{MRI reconstruction \and Trustworthy AI \and Uncertainty quantification \and Explainability \and Adversarial robustness.}
\end{abstract}

%=============================================
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================

Magnetic resonance imaging provides unparalleled soft-tissue contrast for cardiac diagnosis, yet its inherently slow acquisition limits patient throughput and raises healthcare costs~\cite{safari2025}. Acquiring fewer k-space measurements and reconstructing the missing information with deep neural networks has become an established acceleration strategy; architectures spanning U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015}, variational networks~\cite{hammernik2018}, and cascaded data-consistency (DC) models~\cite{schlemper2018} routinely achieve high-fidelity images at four- to eightfold undersampling~\cite{safari2025}.

High average reconstruction quality alone, however, does not suffice for clinical deployment. A trustworthy reconstruction system must reveal \emph{when} it may err (uncertainty quantification), \emph{why} it produces a particular output (explainability), whether it degrades gracefully under perturbation (robustness), and whether downstream diagnostic tasks remain reliable (clinical utility)~\cite{atalik2026tgvn,kaur2022trustworthy}. Although individual facets, such as MC Dropout uncertainty~\cite{gal2016}, deep ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017}, and gradient-based attribution, have been studied in isolation, evaluations that probe multiple trust dimensions simultaneously for MRI reconstruction remain scarce.

This work presents a unified trustworthiness evaluation with five contributions: (1)~a U-Net with cascaded soft DC layers, hyper-parameter-optimised via Bayesian search to 31.9\,dB PSNR at $R{=}4{\times}$; (2)~explainability analysis via saliency maps, Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017}, and integrated gradients~\cite{sundararajan2017}, adapted from classification to reconstruction loss; (3)~a comparative uncertainty study of MC Dropout ($T{=}30$) and deep ensembles ($M{=}3$)~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017} with calibration diagnostics; (4)~robustness evaluation under noise, adversarial attacks~\cite{goodfellow2015explaining,madry2018towards}, MR$\to$CT domain shift, and a DC-layer ablation; (5)~downstream cardiac segmentation evaluation using MM-WHS labels~\cite{zhuang2019mmwhs} with uncertainty--segmentation-failure correlation analysis.

%=============================================
\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%=============================================

\paragraph{Deep MRI reconstruction.}
Supervised methods fall into three families~\cite{safari2025}: end-to-end models such as U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015} that map zero-filled inputs to targets but risk hallucinating structures~\cite{antun2020instabilities}; unrolled optimisation methods including VarNet~\cite{hammernik2018} and MoDL~\cite{aggarwal2019modl}; and DC-layer models~\cite{schlemper2018} that enforce fidelity to acquired k-space measurements. This work adopts a cascaded soft DC design.

\paragraph{Trustworthy AI in medical imaging.}
Kaur et al.~\cite{kaur2022trustworthy} survey trustworthiness desiderata. The TGVN~\cite{atalik2026tgvn} introduces ambiguous-space consistency to suppress hallucination via the forward operator's near-null singular vectors. MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016} approximates variational inference, deep ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017} capture uncertainty through prediction disagreement, and Kendall and Gal~\cite{kendall2017uncertainties} decompose aleatoric from epistemic components. Edupuganti et al.~\cite{edupuganti2021uncertainty} compare MC Dropout with heteroscedastic models for MRI, while Narnhofer et al.~\cite{narnhofer2022bayesian} apply Bayesian inference within variational networks. A direct calibration comparison of MC Dropout and deep ensembles for MRI reconstruction has not been reported.

\paragraph{Explainability and adversarial robustness.}
Attribution methods such as saliency~\cite{simonyan2014deep}, Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017}, and integrated gradients~\cite{sundararajan2017} are established for classification but under-explored for reconstruction, where no class label exists. Antun et al.~\cite{antun2020instabilities} show that small perturbations produce dramatic reconstruction failures, motivating adversarial evaluation with FGSM~\cite{goodfellow2015explaining} and PGD~\cite{madry2018towards}. This work extends this line by testing whether uncertainty can detect such attacks.

%=============================================
\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}
%=============================================

\subsection{Network Architecture and Data Consistency}
Let $\mathbf{x}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{H\times W}$ denote the fully-sampled cardiac MR image and $\mathbf{k}=\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x})$ its 2-D DFT. A binary mask $\mathbf{M}$ retains a subset $\Omega$ of k-space lines, yielding the zero-filled input $\mathbf{x}_0=|\mathcal{F}^{-1}(\mathbf{M}\!\odot\!\mathbf{k})|$. A four-level U-Net predicts a residual correction: $\hat{\mathbf{x}}=\mathbf{x}_0+f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0)$, using Instance Normalisation, LeakyReLU, and spatial dropout ($p{=}0.11$). The output passes through $C{=}3$ learnable soft DC cascades:
\begin{equation}
\hat{K}(k) = \begin{cases}
(1{-}\lambda)\,\hat{K}_{\text{pred}}(k) + \lambda\, K_\Omega(k), & k\in\Omega,\\
\hat{K}_{\text{pred}}(k), & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a sigmoid-gated learnable scalar. Training minimises $\mathcal{L}=\alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSIM}}+(1{-}\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\ell_1}$ with $\alpha{=}0.51$.

\subsection{Explainability, Uncertainty, and Robustness}
Three attribution methods are adapted to reconstruction: saliency maps ($|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}_0}\mathcal{L}|$), Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017} with the reconstruction loss replacing the class logit, and integrated gradients~\cite{sundararajan2017} from a zero baseline. Faithfulness is measured via Pearson correlation with the ground-truth error map (see Appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:xai}).

MC Dropout ($T{=}30$ stochastic passes) following Gal and Ghahramani~\cite{gal2016} yields pixel-wise mean and standard deviation. A deep ensemble of $M{=}3$ independently initialised networks~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017} provides an alternative uncertainty estimate. Both are evaluated via Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Area Under the Sparsification Error curve (AUSE), and Pearson $r$ with absolute error.

Perturbations include k-space Gaussian noise ($\sigma_n\!\in\![0.01,0.3]$), FGSM~\cite{goodfellow2015explaining} and PGD~\cite{madry2018towards} ($K{=}7$) at $\epsilon\!\in\!\{0.005,\ldots,0.1\}$, and cross-domain evaluation on CT images from MM-WHS~\cite{zhuang2019mmwhs}. A DC ablation (withholding k-space and mask at inference) isolates the DC layer's contribution.

\section{Experiments and Results}\label{sec:results}
%=============================================

\subsection{Setup}
The MM-WHS cardiac dataset~\cite{zhuang2019mmwhs} provides $256{\times}256$ magnitude images with eight-class segmentation labels: 1\,738/254/236 MR and 3\,389/382/484 CT slices (train/val/test). Representative samples are shown in Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:dataset}. K-space is simulated via 2-D FFT with retrospective random Cartesian undersampling retaining the central 8\,\% of lines. A ten-trial Bayesian search (Optuna) selected: learning rate $1.6{\times}10^{-3}$, batch size 16, 32 base features, dropout $p{=}0.11$, three DC cascades, $\alpha{=}0.51$, weight decay $3.4{\times}10^{-6}$. Training runs for 100 epochs with cosine annealing. The three ensemble members share the architecture but differ in weight initialisation and data-loader ordering.

Gradient-based attributions reveal how the network achieves these gains. Saliency maps concentrate on cardiac boundaries, which are the high-frequency structures most degraded by k-space truncation. Grad-CAM heatmaps across five U-Net stages show hierarchical attention: shallow encoder layers attend to local edges, while deeper decoder stages capture global anatomical context (Appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:xai}). All three attribution methods correlate positively with the ground-truth error map, confirming that learned attributions are faithful to actual failure modes. This adaptation of classification-oriented attribution to a reconstruction task, using reconstruction loss as the gradient target instead of a class logit, validates that the network allocates representational capacity to clinically relevant structures.

The methods offer complementary strengths: ensembles for calibrated intervals, MC Dropout for ordinal error ranking. Table~\ref{tab:robust} shows that PGD degrades PSNR more severely than FGSM at every budget, as expected from an iterative attack (see also Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:adversarial}). The DC layer limits damage by enforcing fidelity to the original acquired k-space, which remains unaffected by image-domain perturbations. The MR-trained model applied to CT data yields 0.9\,dB lower PSNR but 77\% higher uncertainty, appropriately signalling reduced confidence on out-of-distribution inputs (Appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:crossdomain}).

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%=============================================

All three attribution methods correlate positively with reconstruction error, confirming that the U-Net encodes clinically meaningful features. Saliency concentrates on cardiac boundaries while Grad-CAM reveals hierarchical reasoning from local edges to global anatomy. MC Dropout and ensembles offer complementary uncertainty: MC Dropout achieves stronger pixel-level correlation ($r{=}0.59$) and tighter sparsification for per-pixel masking, while ensembles yield lower ECE (0.017 vs.\ 0.026) for calibrated confidence intervals. A clinical deployment could combine ensemble disagreement for slice-level quality gating with MC Dropout for pixel-level uncertainty overlay.

The DC layer enforces fidelity to measured k-space regardless of network predictions, so image-domain adversarial perturbations cannot alter acquired measurements. Disabling DC collapses PSNR by 8.1\,dB, making it the single largest factor in reconstruction quality, paralleling the TGVN~\cite{atalik2026tgvn} principle of separating trusted from untrusted information.

The 77\% uncertainty increase on out-of-distribution CT data demonstrates practical OOD detection. Downstream segmentation evaluation moves beyond pixel metrics: Dice preservation at $R{=}2{-}4{\times}$ confirms artefacts do not catastrophically propagate, while the positive uncertainty--segmentation-failure correlation ($r{=}0.19$) suggests uncertainty maps can predict diagnostic degradation spatially.

Simulated single-coil Cartesian undersampling does not capture multi-coil clinical complexity. The three-member ensemble is small; larger ensembles may improve calibration. The moderate uncertainty--segmentation correlation ($r{=}0.19$) leaves room for improvement via heteroscedastic loss~\cite{edupuganti2021uncertainty} or uncertainty-guided adaptive DC. Future work should extend to multi-coil data and prospectively undersampled acquisitions.
